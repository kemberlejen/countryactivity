Tradition

The Mint Tea Ritual is a cherished Moroccan tradition that goes beyond the simple act of drinking tea. It is a symbol of hospitality, friendship, and warmth deeply ingrained in Moroccan culture. The ritual begins with the preparation of a flavorful blend of green tea leaves, fresh mint leaves, and sugar. The tea is brewed in a teapot, poured into small glasses, and then poured back into the teapot multiple times to mix the flavors and create a frothy texture. This process, known as “high pouring,” is an art form in itself. The tea is then served to guests, who sip it slowly, savoring the refreshing taste and engaging in conversation. The Mint Tea Ritual is a beautiful expression of Moroccan hospitality, creating a sense of connection and conviviality.

The Hammam Tradition in Morocco is a centuries-old bathing ritual that is both a cleansing experience and a cherished social activity. The hammam, or public bathhouse, provides a space for people to gather, relax, and rejuvenate. The ritual involves a series of steps, starting with a steam session to open the pores and cleanse the skin. This is followed by a vigorous exfoliation using a special glove called a kessa and a traditional black soap made from olives. The body is thoroughly scrubbed to remove impurities and dead skin cells. Afterward, cool water is used to rinse off the soap and refresh the body. The Hammam Tradition is not only a physical cleansing but also a time for relaxation, socializing, and self-care.

Food

B'ssara soup of dried broad beans is traditionally served for breakfast, topped with a swirl of olive oil, a sprinkling of cumin and bread fresh from the oven.

Tagine is the clay cooking pot with a conical lid that gives its name to a myriad of dishes. Tagines can be seen bubbling away at every roadside café, are found in top notch restaurants and in every home, and are always served with bread.

With its long Atlantic and Mediterranean coasts, Morocco boasts a rich array of fish dishes. Chermoula is a combination of herbs and spices used as a marinade before grilling over coals, and as a dipping sauce.

During the holy month of Ramadan, the fast is broken at sunset each day with a steaming bowl of harira soup. Rich with tomatoes, lentils, chickpeas and lamb, it is finished off with a squeeze of lemon juice and some chopped coriander, and served with a sticky sweet pretzel called chebakkiya.

Beef or lamb mince with garlic, fresh coriander and parsley, cinnamon and ground coriander is rolled into balls and cooked in a tomato and onion sauce. Just before the dish is ready, eggs are cracked into depressions in the sauce and soon cook to perfection

Couscous is a fine wheat pasta traditionally rolled by hand. It is steamed over a stew of meat and vegetables. To serve, the meat is covered by a pyramid of couscous, the vegetables are pressed into the sides and the sauce served separately. It is often garnished with a sweet raisin preserve, or in the Berber tradition, with a bowl of buttermilk.

Maakouda is a savory Moroccan Potato Beignet, deep fried in oil and usually served in a small baguette.

Zaalouk is a popular Moroccan cooked salad made of eggplant and tomatoes with garlic and warm seasonings including cumin, paprika, and fresh cilantro.

B’stilla, also called pastilla, is a savory-sweet pie with precious saffron in a crisp phyllo-style wrapping.

Moroccan mint tea, also known as atay bi nana, is a highly valued and cherished tradition in Morocco.
